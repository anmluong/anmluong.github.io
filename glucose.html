Glucose Control Interface Redesign

Increasing accuracy and responsiveness in patient glucose monitoring

Role: UX & UI design

Tool: Figma

Platform: Desktop & mobile

Time: 3 months

This was a group project for my Human Machine Interface course in the Fall 2019 semester. This interface is used in intensive care units (ICUs) to monitor patients’ blood glucose (BG) levels according to hospital protocol. Nurses take BG readings at time intervals specified by protocols, enter the readings into the interface, and administer the proper dosage of insulin to the patient. This dosage is calculated by the interface system based on the BG entry.

As the design lead of our 4-person team, my main contributions were the hierarchical task analyses, heuristic evaluation of the existing system, and final interface mockup.

Problem

Medical errors as a result of the poor display and information entry of the glucose control interface will lead to serious patient health issues. Further, delayed action in the time-sensitive glucose control process will hinder patient recovery.

Guiding question

How might we redesign the glucose control interface to facilitate accurate and responsive patient care in a time-sensitive ICU environment?

Solution

A blood glucose monitoring interface that provides greater visibility, context, and error prevention to ensure patient health and recovery. Main features include a clear alarm indicator, streamlined one-screen process for glucose reading and insulin administration, visual display of patient history, and mobile application counterpart.

Research

User analysis

User groups

Since nurses are the only clinicians using this interface, we identified our two user groups as the following:

Oncoming nurses
  • Not as vulnerable to fatigue but need time to adjust to the system and review patient statuses
  • Would benefit from greater visibility and understandability of patient history
Outgoing nurses
  • More acclimated to system and patients but more prone to mental lapses due to long 8-12 hour shifts
  • Would benefit from error prevention and error recovery

System analysis

Hierarchical task analysis

To familiarize ourselves with the system and understand fundamental functionalities, I performed the following simplified hierarchical task analysis and highlighted tasks that corresponded to the following pain points.

There's no condition checking in the system, so nurses must reference hospital protocol documents to determine appropriate blood glucose reading ranges. This puts an additional burden on nurses that may result in medical errors.

Further, nurses are unable to view patient history during BG reading entry, which would prevent them from identifying outlying or irregular readings for individual patients.



Heuristic evaluation

Once we were acclimated to the glucose control interface, I led the heuristic evaluation of the existing system. A small sample of the usability problems are included below.

Visibility of system status: Time until next BG reading is not highly visible, which impedes timely patient care.

Visibility of system status: BG alarm text is not prominent and accompanying alarm sound may not be heard from other patient rooms, which also impedes timely patient care.

Match between system and real world: Dialogue such as “Start New Drip” for patient login and “Resume” for nurse login is not intuitive, which may slow nurses down.

Error prevention: Nurses may be susceptible to information entry errors, but the system doesn’t handle error conditions.

Recognition rather than recall: Context of patient history is not available while logging BG reading and viewing insulin dosage, which means nurses may not detect irregular levels or trends in the readings.

Insights

From our system analysis and discussions with our "users" (TAs), we determined the following to be the most important information to nurses that we must visually prioritize in the interface redesign:

  • Basic patient information and patient history
  • Time until next blood glucose reading
  • Whether BG levels are in an acceptable range
  • Insulin dosage based on BG levels

Feedback & reflection

Although the first iteration improved upon the existing system by increasing visibility of relevant information, it was still functionally very similar. These were highlighted changes to be made based on "user" (TA) feedback and team reflection:

  • Prioritize visibility of BG alarm over patient identifying information since the BG alarm is time-sensitive
  • Give greater prominence to BG entry on the screen since it's the primary task of the interface
  • Condense the process of logging BG reading to one screen so that all required information is available at the same time

Design

Patient home screen

Visibility of patient information: Identifying information and patient status is clearly displayed to minimize adjustment period for nurses starting a new shift

Understandability of patient history: Visual display of last BG reading paired with context of acceptable ranges and extended BG history improves nurses' understanding of current patient status

Disabled blood glucose entry: Prevents nurse from erroneously entering BG readings before alarm goes off

Mobile patient display: Allows nurses to view information on the go, increasing responsiveness

Patient alert screen

Visibility of blood glucose alarm: Redundancy in the color, icon, remaining time, and snooze button clearly indicates that the nurse needs to take a BG reading

Streamlined blood glucose and insulin process: BG entry and insulin dosage administration are done in one page, so nurses avoid spending unnecessary time navigating through multiple screens

Error prevention: System checks BG entries against patient history and realistic BG threshold levels to prevent nurses from making errors

Mobile overview of all alarms: Allows nurses to remain aware of and anticipate upcoming BG readings

Next steps

We didn't have the opportunity to interact with medical professionals, which limited our research efforts with observations and interviews. Further, due to the nature of this course, we did not conduct user testing. Although this redesign is an improvement upon the existing system based on heuristics and design principles, we don't know whether the system would improve patient care without more extensive research or improve accuracy and responsiveness without testing.

Based on feedback by course instructors, next steps for another iteration of this design include:

  • Reconsidering the disabling of the BG panel when there’s still time left on the BG alarm so nurses aren’t completely restricted from entering BG readings
  • Simplifying the information displayed in the BG History graph to minimize clutter
  • Reconsidering the display of patient status ("Stable" or "Unstable") to avoid alarming patients and patients' families in the ICU room
