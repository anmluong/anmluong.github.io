ARChef

Using AR in recipe tutorials to break down the learning curve for college cooking

Contributions: User interviews, affinity diagram, IA, user flow, wireframes, mockups

Platform: Mobile

Tools: Figma, Balsamiq

Time: January 2019 - April 2019 (+ one week in August 2020)

ARChef (Augmented Reality Chef) is a mobile app that builds upon existing impersonal cooking applications by incorporating visualizations of ingredients and kitchen actions onto user workspaces to simulate in-person recipe tutorials.

This was a semester-long group project for my Human-Computer Interaction course. Teams were allowed to pursue any problem and solution without considering technical constraints. I led our 5-person team from research through mockups. After reflecting on the project, I went back to simplify our features and improve the overall interface. See the redesign here.

Narrowing the problem space

Our starting point

Since we were given such an open-ended assignment, we wanted to focus on a specific topic. We were all sophomores at the time transitioning out of meal plans, so we decided on college cooking.

Students' perspectives on cooking

We needed to understand cooking from a student lens outside of our own, so each of us conducted interviews with students of various cooking abilities. From our interview notes, we each created work activity notes that were compiled into a work activity affinity diagram to inform potential design requirements. These were some of the common themes we saw.

Meet Achilles

To guide our design, my teammate used our research to create our persona: Achilles Stamos, a UVA sophomore transitioning from his reliance on dining hall food.

Goals

  • Become an independent adult
  • Live a healthy, balanced lifestyle

Behaviors

  • Chooses most convenient option, whether it’s overpriced campus food or microwaveable meals
  • Depends on their smartphone

Challenges

  • Lacks kitchen knowledge and cooking experience
  • Can’t dedicate extensive time blocks to cooking

The challenge

Our research led us to the following problem statement: Many college-aged students transition into independence from family-provided meals and college meal plans with limited or no cooking ability due to the intimidating learning curve of cooking. How might we create an app that eases college students into home cooking in a novel manner?

Ideating

Connecting insights with ideas

We matched each of our insights with a corresponding mobile app feature idea. A couple of these are shown below. One of our key features with augmented reality was inspired by Pokémon Go and Snapchat's uses of 3D visualizations. From a functional stance, this aspect would provide a visual and personal guide to our student users. From an emotional stance, students would feel less intimidated because of the entertainment factor.

Insight: Students learn from friends, family, and visual tutorials

Feature: Augmented visualizations onto user workspace so users can mirror instructions

Insight: Students don’t have the experience to understand when something is done cooking

Feature: Image recognition through smartphone cameras to alert cook of error or completion

Insight: Students learn best from incremental and foundational learning

Feature: Optional foundational tutorials (e.g. knife skills)

Creating wireframes for student feedback

Based on compiled sketches from all team members, I created the following wireframes for classroom critiques.

Introducing different recipe modes

To address our concerns and our classmates' concerns on the frequent switches between using a phone and cooking, we decided to implement voice commands and create a Still Mode and Active AR Mode.

Still Mode: Hands-off, projects the visualizations onto a user-captured still image of the kitchen workspace so that students can set their phone down and follow along while cooking

Active AR Mode: Hands-on, use image recognition to determine if user is ready to move to the next recipe step or if they're at risk of making a mistake (i.e. burning, overcooking)

Design

From our classmates' feedback and our concept revisions, my teammates and I built out these mockups.

Personalization: Onboarding questionnaire allows students to customize their experience, eliminating any additional barriers to starting the learning process for cooking.

Financial benefits: Cost comparisons between ARChef recipes and local restaurants incentivize home cooking.

Visual guidance: The AR tutorial, in combination with image recognition technology, walks the student through each step of the recipe.

Cooking education: To further emphasize foundational skills, students can utilize tutorials to reach their personal cooking goals.

Revisiting our work

We had a lot of fun playing around with the idea of incorporating AR into the culinary space, and I wanted to revisit ARChef a year later to see how it could be improved in terms of organization and visuals.

Examining information architecture

Looking back on this project, we rushed towards wireframes and mockups without thoroughly considering how our features would be organized. I created the following IA of my team's final design.

From this, I realized how many features ended up appearing as second thoughts rather than intentionally incorporated features. I revised the IA to emphasize the educational aspect of ARChef and expanded the recipe view.

Detailing the AR feature with wireframes

In our original design mockups, we didn't flesh out the AR tutorial flow, so to understand what screens were needed for our main task of following a recipe, I created sketches that I turned into the following wire flows.

From the recipe page, students begin the tutorial through the video view. To project visualizations onto their workspace (like guiding lines for chopping), students can select the AR view. For steps that have the "Check" option, students can ask the app to double check their progress, whether it's checking to see if a pan is too crowded or if an ingredient is diced finely enough. There is also an option for a list view of the recipe steps.

Redesign

Main screens

This redesign incorporates incremental learning through color-coded levels of cooking that classify both recipes and tutorials. From Discover, students can search and bookmark new recipes. Under My recipes, students can access their previously saved recipes. They can also build their foundational skills through tutorials under Learn.

Recipe tutorial

Before beginning a recipe, students scan their workspace to have ARChef check off the ingredients list. They then start with the video tutorial and have the option of using AR mode to see 2D visualizations of the steps on their workspace or asking for Help to make sure they're following the recipe correctly.

What I learned

It's important to avoid working in a silo. Since we had left the AR concept so open-ended at the end of the semester, I struggled with how to build out the feature until I reached out to other students to get their ideas and feedback. Continuously checking in with others made the redesign process so much more fun and helped me avoid isolating the design to my own ideas and biases.
